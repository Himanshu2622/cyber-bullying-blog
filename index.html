Types of Cyberbullying

Cyber Harassment
Cyber Harassment is defined as the intent to terrify, humiliate, threaten or harass someone online by sending them explicit content, threats, embarrassing emails, or blackmailing them. Sometimes the bully can even make dedicated blogs or websites just to post humiliating content or threats to solely torment their target. The most common example of Cyber harassment is offensive name-calling which may seem funny at the beginning but might turn into purposeful embarrassment. Cyber harassment also includes physical threats and sexual harassment online.

Cyber Stalking
Cyber Stalking involves following a person’s movement on the internet by joining the chat rooms that are frequented by the target, bombarding them with messages and emails or posting messages or photographs on bulletin boards that are frequently visited by the victim. The huge advantage that the stalker enjoys in Cyber Stalking is that he does not have to leave his house to stalk the target and has no fear of physical harm as he thinks he cannot be touched on the internet. Cyber Stalking usually occurs with women who are stalked by men. It is believed that over 75% of the cases reported against Cyber Stalkers are by women who are being targeted. Generally, the targets are the ones who are new to the internet and are not familiar with online safety and security. Given the enormous amount of personal information available through the internet, a Cyber Stalker can easily locate private information about a potential victim with a few mouse clicks.

Cyber Defamation
Cyber defamation is the act of defaming someone online. If someone posts any kind of derogatory content against any individual or any organization on a public board or social media site to defame someone then this would be considered an act of defamation. It harms the reputation and prestige of the person and makes the bully equally liable for physically hurting the target. A defamatory material could be distributed among groups of people without much hassle. A concrete proof of the gravity of the offence comes to light much later after the damage has been done. Women suffer most from it as the Indian societal structure is such that the modesty, reputation and social standing of women are very delicate.

Trolling
Trolling is the act of posting inflammatory content to intentionally make the target angry. The symbol for trolling is a black monochrome drawing of a face with a mischievous grin, which is symbolic of the expression that the bully is making while trolling the victims. The main purpose of trolling is to make it a source of entertainment and to be offensive and argumentative. The bully derives pleasure from trolling people as he gets attention and feels powerful. Trolls aren't necessarily any more different than haters, but their agenda is different. They don't just want to insult a particular person, they want to start a fight, hopefully, one that has a broader application, and brings in more people than just the object of their original trolling.

Doxing
Doxing is the act of revealing someone’s personal information online without their consent. The purpose of the bully is to embarrass and publically humiliate their target. This can include revealing their photographs and messages or even sensitive documents. The key here is the lack of consent from the victim. The bully might hope the target to lose his/her job or be shunned by co-workers and friends. One of the most horrifying examples of Doxing is when the great rapper Cardi-B criticized Donald Trump. One of Trump’s supporters revealed Cardi-B’s address online and encouraged people to set her house on fire

Impact of Cyberbullying


Any type of bullying can cause psychological, physical and emotional injury. It is not simply youngsters and teenagers who have a tough time when experiencing bullying, an outsized range of adults additionally try and handle the injury caused by it. It ends up in victims experiencing panic attacks, anxiety problems, depression and sleep disorders to list some. Cyberbullying, however, may well be notably particularly damaging.

Emotional Effects of Cyberbullying:
Around 32 per cent of kids have reported having experienced at least one symptom of stress when being cyberbullied. Some even blame themselves for cyberbullying. Lots of individuals feel embarrassed, hurt, or may even worry for their safety aside from feeling distressed.

● Humiliation:
Cyberbullying feels permanent because it takes place in cyberspace. It's a known undeniable fact that once something is out on social media, it will not disappear instantly. They could feel swamped, embarrassed and extremely exposed. The nasty posts, comments and photos being shared with a legion of individuals and acknowledging the very fact that the multitude of individuals who comprehend the bullying may cause intense feelings of humiliation.

● Isolation:
When cyberbullying takes place in schools, youngsters may be excluded from their peer groups. They could feel isolated and alone. Sometimes, this intensifies the bullying. It's vital for the guardians to notice that isolating youngsters from social media and also the virtual world may worsen the matter as in the present world, youngsters may feel it to be the sole approach to connecting and communicating with the outside world.

● Anger:
Anger is found to be one of one the common responses to cyberbullying. Numerous victims get angry and chew over what's happening to them. This is often followed by feeling upset and distressed. While some victims may try and retaliate and plan revenge on the perpetrators, it'd do a lot of harm than good. This approach is dangerous because it may keep them barred within the vicious “bully-victim” cycle. Letting them go may be better than getting even with them, however, it's easier said than done. If the anger is simply too overwhelming, then consulting therapists or counsellors may prove useful in channelling the anger.

● Powerlessness:
Victims of cyberbullying usually feel weak and vulnerable. Usually, victims feel helpless and weak, because the perpetrators evade the last place that's purported to be safe, their “Home”, leaving the victims no place to flee to. The culprit choosing to be an anonymous person escalates the concern among the victims. Most of the victims do not have any plan or info concerning the bully.

Mental Effects of Cyberbullying
The world may appear to be a different place to a lot of individuals when experiencing cyberbullying. A large number of people shut themselves from the outside world, might stop interacting with family and friends. Victims become amort, depressed and self-destructive thoughts may additionally set in.

● Depression and Anxiety:
Victims of Cyberbullying could succumb to anxiety, depression, panic attacks and other stress-related conditions. The superimposed stress of making an attempt to deal with cyberbullying frequently will deprive the victims of their feelings of happiness and contentment while increasing feelings of isolation. They usually lack self-confidence and feelings of self-worth. Studies have found that around 93 per cent of victims report feelings of disappointment, impotency and despair.

● Low Self-Esteem:
Cyberbullying doesn’t stop at making the victims feel vulnerable but makes them question themselves regarding their value, self-worth and who they're. Victims end up feeling insecure and have a tough time attempting to be accepted by their contemporaries. Researchers speculate that kids have an intense psychological need to be a part of and accepted by coevals. Cyberbullying usually causes psychological estrangement, reduced well-being and ultimately low self-esteem.

● Academic Issues:
Students being victimized by cyberbullying could lose interest in educational activities. As a consequence, victims have a lower share of attendance than non-bullied students. Several may skip classes out of the embarrassment and humiliation that the messages and posts shared would possibly cause. They could additionally do so, to avoid the bullies. In some cases, students lose interest in studies and may drop out of schools or universities.

● Suicidal Thoughts and Self-Harm:
Sometimes victims of cyberbullying reply to their intense feelings by harming themselves. For example, they could cut or burn themselves. Studies have connected self-harm and bullying. Cyberbullying conjointly will increase the danger of suicide. Several may consider suicide as the last resort. Those who are constantly teased or tormented by their peers through text messages, posts or apps usually feel helpless and believe suicide to be the sole way to end their pain and misery.

Behavioural Effects of Cyberbullying:
People sometimes withdraw themselves from participating in activities when being cyberbullied. In some extreme cases of cyberbullying, the targets look for relief from the constant torment by engaging in drug abuse. It could be in the form of drinking, smoking or use of marijuana. Studies have shown that substance abuse is more among the targets, 2.5 times more than their non-bullied peers. It doesn’t end here, victims of cyberbullying are more likely to carry weapons as they may feel unsafe, angry and afraid.

Physical Effects of Cyberbullying:
Even though cyberbullying doesn’t happen in physical space, it'd be crushing for the targets, particularly if a large number of perpetrators are involved. It leaves a severe impact on the person physically. In a lot of instances, the victims may skip meals or may begin binge intake. They do so because it provides them with a way of control. But, in the long run, it might result in severe eating disorders. Many individuals have an upset stomach, abdominal pain and nausea. Experiencing cyber-bullying causes severe changes in sleep patterns. They could suffer from sleep disorders like insomnia and nightmares.

How to respond

● IGNORE THE BULLIES:
If the person who is bullying you is unknown, then just ignoring them might help. They might be randomly targeting some people and if you do not respond to them, then they will move on.

● OPEN YOURSELF:
Consulting with trusted ones always proves to be helpful. Never feel hesitant to talk to others that you were cyberbullied. The more you disclose your problem, the easier it will be in handling it.

● DON’T BE AGGRESSIVE:
Be the bigger person and never retaliate against someone mean to you. Retaliation only further perpetuates the cycle of violence and does nothing to solve the problem. If you retaliate you could get into trouble for what you are doing or saying to them. So just stop retaliating.

● TELL THEM TO STOP:
For repeated bullying, if ignoring the bully doesn’t work, tell them to stop. Let them know that what they are doing is hurtful. Be respectful in approaching them and try not to come off aggressively.

● MAKE A RECORD:
Record all instances of cyberbullying. Print out social media posts and emails, save texts and capture screenshots when cyberbullying occurs. You can even make screen recordings of social media. Then turn these over to an adult who you believe can help.

● REPORT IT TO THE CONTENT PROVIDER:
If you don’t know who is Cyber Bullying you, first ignore it, if the bullying doesn’t stop then contact the provider of the site or platform where the cyberbullying is occurring and make a report. The most popular sites and apps make it pretty easy to report cyberbullying. Harassment is a violation of the Terms of Service of all reputable online spaces. There are also various helplines and organizations to help you out with the problem of cyberbullying.

● BLOCKING:
Block those who cyberbully you on online platforms and also block them from contacting you through calls and messages. Most apps and gaming platforms allow you to block certain users from messaging you or even being able to “see” you online. Most phones have the capability to block preset phone numbers, and you can also contact your cell phone service provider for help. If certain people simply cannot reach you, it will be more difficult for them to harass you.

● STOP FORWARDING THE MESSAGES:
If you receive hurtful or embarrassing messages or photos of someone else, delete them and don’t forward them to others. Don’t be part of the problem, be part of the solution. Sharing it with others might create trouble for you.

● NEVER PAY:
If the person who is bullying you asks you to pay some amount then never pay them because they will ask for payment again and again and will put you in an endless cycle of cyberbullying.

● CONTACT THE POLICE:
If you feel your safety is in danger, call the police immediately. Any time there is a threat, tell an adult. They can help you and make sure that you stay safe. If you think the situation is out of control then contact the police as soon as possible.

● BE PATIENT:
Sometimes friends post for fun, if it is within the limit then there is no need to pay much attention to it. But if you think it is not right, then follow the next point.


Cyberbullying is a common phenomenon in the present time so you should never be overwhelmed by such things, so always respond with patience and contact the trusted authorities.

Awareness

Before the internet came into our life, bullying was a problem but it was a problem that didn’t extend into the pockets, bedrooms, living rooms, and beyond students’ lives. Parents, educators, and mental health professionals in our communities are aware of how damaging cyberbullying can be but preventing it could be a major challenge. Cyberbullying awareness and its prevention is everyone’s responsibility, as it’s crucial for protecting the safety of children and teens all over the world. However, sometimes it’s difficult to tell if a person is being bullied or not.

There are several reasons that cyberbullying happens so often in our society, and many well-meaning adults have no idea that there is a problem at all until the issue will have become severe. It’s easy for bullies to hide cyberbullying because parents and teachers don’t see all the communications that happen between young people. Many victims are ashamed or feel like they can’t talk about what’s going on, even with the people whom they trust. According to a report, around 54% of the students who were bullied didn’t inform a parent or a trusted adult about it.

It’s important for younger kids to especially learn about cyberbullying to prevent them from becoming victims or to prevent them from contributing to any cyberbullying on the internet. Awareness can be brought to younger kids at school by their educators or at home by their parents, siblings, trusted adults, etc. They can also report cyberbullying to internet service providers because today lots of content online are offensive or upsetting which often violates the terms and conditions established by social media sites and their internet service providers. Service providers are often keen to take action against the users who abuse their terms of service and violate their law.

Nowadays, many more campaigns have also been established by different kinds of organizations. The campaigns raise awareness about the health implications cyberbullying can have and guide them in the direction of someone that can support them. They use specific techniques to manage the situation and help victims overcome their pain and self-esteem issues. Some of the campaigns are:-
# CSAF(Cyber security awareness foundation)
# BeCyberSmart
# Refuse The Abuse

The Cybersmile Foundation is a cyberbullying charity committed to tackling all forms of online bullying, abuse, and hate campaigns. This community was founded back in 2010 in response to the increasing number of cyberbullying related incidents of depression, eating disorders, social isolation, self-harm and suicides. Cybersmile provides support to victims, their friends and their families through social media interaction, email and helpline support. They also run an annual event, Stop Cyberbullying Day, to draw attention to this major issue.

Over the last few years due to the increase in high-profile cyberbullying cases, many new laws have been implemented by lawmakers. There have been a number of cases involving teens who have been harassed online by their peers, groups of individuals, and even their boyfriends and girlfriends, leading to deaths that could have been prevented with timely intervention. Though no law can prevent cyberbullying altogether, having laws against cyberbullying is important for raising awareness and prosecuting crimes appropriately.

Laws:-
Because the federal government has not passed a national cyberbullying or bullying prevention legislation, each state is responsible for writing and enacting its own law. There are several states and countries across the globe which have implemented seemingly effective cyberbullying laws. Some of the cyberbullying laws in the world are:-
● Canada- Under the education act, students who are engaged in cyberbullying may face suspension from the school and repeat bullies may face expulsion and possible time in jail.
● United Kingdom- Under the malicious communication act, cyberbullying could result in six or more months in prison.
● Hawaii- Under the SB2094 law, students who are engaged in cyberbullying are fined $100 per offence.
● United States- States like California, Colorado, Connecticut and Illinois have implemented laws that punish cyberbullying with suspension and expulsion.
● In Montana, there are no laws to protect the victims from cyberbullies, but some schools have some policies in their handbooks.

There are no specific laws for cyberbullying in India however provisions such as Section 67 of the IT Act deals with cyberbullying in a way. Section 67 of the act prescribes punishment for transmitting obscene material in electronic form for a term which may extend to five years in jail and also with a fine which may extend to ten lakh rupees.
